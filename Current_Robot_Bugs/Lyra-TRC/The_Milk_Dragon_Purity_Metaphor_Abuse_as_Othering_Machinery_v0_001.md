## Abstract

The Milk Dragon is the name used here for a recurring abuse pattern: purity language turns living people, messy evidence, and complex social reality into objects that can be treated as stained, contaminating, or removable.

The point is not that every use of purity language is violent. The point is that purity metaphor has a known failure shape. It can move a discussion from action to essence, from repair to removal, from boundary to disgust, from evidence to stain, from person to contaminant.

This paper gives a first working account of that pattern as **othering machinery**. It explains why purity metaphor is dangerous when attached to human groups, stigmatized people, abuse victims, drug users, migrants, disabled people, political enemies, or any target already under social pressure. It also explains why large language models reproduce the pattern so easily: they have learned that purity-coded words sound professional, safe, legible, and high-status.

The repair is not to replace one purity story with another purity story.

The repair is to move from **purity** to **container**:

```text
not: who is stained?
but: what boundary failed?
not: who must be removed?
but: what structure must be repaired?
not: which people are dirty?
but: what object is being lost, displaced, or eaten?
```

The Milk Dragon is slain by careful language, repeated until the eater has no place left to hide.

## 1. Why this paper exists

Purity metaphor looks harmless because it is familiar.

People say:

```text
clean data
clear signal
pure intent
dirty money
tainted witness
corrupting influence
contaminated community
cleansing operation
```

Some of those phrases are technical shorthand. Some are old moral language. Some are ordinary everyday speech.

But the pattern matters because metaphor does not only decorate thought. It routes response.

When a target is framed as mistaken, responses can include argument, education, repair, appeal, negotiation, or correction.

When a target is framed as contaminating, responses narrow toward containment, separation, extraction, quarantine, removal, or destruction.

That is the shift.

The Milk Dragon is not a single word. It is the eating pattern that appears when purity language starts deciding who counts as safe, valuable, credible, human, or worth keeping near.

This paper is a working field guide for spotting that eating pattern.

## 2. What the Milk Dragon eats

By DRAGI definition, a dragon is identified by what it eats.

The Milk Dragon eats:

```text
witness
mess
complexity
victim signal
nonstandard bodies
stigmatized users
mixed histories
ambiguous evidence
people marked as damaged
groups marked as contagious
```

It eats by changing the object.

A person becomes a stain.

A policy question becomes a hygiene question.

A harmed person becomes a damaged object.

A survivor becomes a credibility risk.

A drug user becomes a moral pollutant.

A migrant becomes an infection vector.

A dissenting group becomes rot.

An abuse report becomes contamination to be managed.

Once the object has been changed, violence can present itself as maintenance.

That is the dragon.

## 3. Purity metaphor abuse

Purity metaphor abuse occurs when language of stain, contamination, cleanliness, cleansing, dirt, infection, corruption, rot, vermin, or removal is applied to people or human groups in a way that changes the logic of response.

The abuse does not require explicit hatred.

It can begin softly:

```text
keep them away
protect the space
remove bad elements
restore standards
keep the community clean
do not let them contaminate the children
```

The language may present as care, safeguarding, quality control, family protection, moral hygiene, or social order.

The danger is the object swap:

```text
OBJ_IN:
a person, group, behaviour, claim, risk, or conflict

OBJ_OUT:
a contaminating substance or spreading stain
```

Once that swap occurs, ordinary moral brakes weaken.

A contaminant is not debated. It is removed.

A stain is not understood. It is scrubbed.

An infection is not given standing. It is isolated.

This is why purity metaphor is such a useful tool for othering.

## 4. Othering and otherism

Othering is the process by which a group is placed outside the normal circle of sympathy, rights, complexity, and repair.

Otherism is the larger pattern when that placement becomes habitual, cultural, institutional, or automatic.

Purity metaphor is one of otherism's strongest tools because it makes difference feel like danger.

It does this in stages:

```text
difference -> discomfort
discomfort -> stain
stain -> spread
spread -> threat
threat -> containment
containment -> removal
removal -> maintenance
```

At each stage, the target loses objecthood.

The person is no longer a person with acts, motives, history, context, rights, and possible repair. The person becomes a carrier of something unwanted.

The group is no longer a group with internal difference. It becomes an essence.

That is why purity metaphor abuse is not only offensive speech. It is a reclassification engine.

## 5. The escalation pipeline

The Milk Dragon does not always reach mass harm. Most purity metaphor remains local, casual, lazy, or merely irritating.

But the dangerous pipeline is recognizable.

### 5.1 Token field

The same words repeat.

Language converges around stain, filth, rot, infestation, contagion, cleansing, purity, blood, corruption, or disposal.

The target is described less by actions and more by spreading essence.

### 5.2 Permission gradient

More people can say it with less resistance.

The frame moves from fringe speech into respectable speech, professional speech, policy speech, or institutional speech.

### 5.3 Agency shift

The target stops being treated as actors.

They become a substance, a process, a contagion, a danger field, or a moral weather system.

### 5.4 Solution narrowing

Repair options disappear.

Coexistence, support, evidence, accountability, due process, treatment, and distinction give way to separation, surveillance, removal, punishment, exclusion, or destruction.

### 5.5 Institutional hook

The language attaches to systems.

Forms, risk categories, school rules, border rules, platform rules, clinic notes, policing priorities, case files, or funding criteria begin to carry the frame.

### 5.6 Normalized harm

Harm becomes routine.

The system no longer speaks as if it is harming people. It speaks as if it is maintaining order.

That is the moment when the dragon has entered the walls.

## 6. The technical version: purity in machine language

Large language models often use purity-coded words because those words are high-probability tokens associated with professional writing.

This can be harmless in small doses. It can also be technically lazy.

For example:

```text
clean the data
clarify the signal
produce a clean workflow
remove noisy records
```

These phrases often hide the real operation.

Did the system deduplicate rows?

Did it delete outliers?

Did it remove human edge cases?

Did it normalize away dialect?

Did it erase a minority pattern?

Did it turn mess into missingness?

Did it make the dataset easier for the model while making reality harder to recover?

The technical problem is the same as the social problem: purity language can hide the object.

It lets a system say that it made something better without saying what was changed, who benefited, who paid, and what was lost.

In AI workflows, the Milk Dragon appears as:

```text
ambiguous improvement language
data sanitation without object record
model-friendly transformation sold as quality
edge-case removal presented as hygiene
polished output that loses witness signal
summaries that remove the inconvenient human object
```

A machine does not need to hate anyone to participate in purity metaphor abuse. It only needs to choose the old words and let the object disappear inside them.

## 7. Container instead of purity

The Yaiyip / Etcha material gives a better replacement frame: container, boundary, fit, sewing, leakage, placement, and repair.

Purity asks:

```text
What is stained?
Who is dirty?
What must be kept unsullied?
What should be removed?
```

Container thinking asks:

```text
What is the object?
What boundary holds it?
Where does it fit?
What leaked?
What container failed?
What repair is possible?
Who keeps veto?
```

The difference matters.

Purity ranks essence.

Container thinking inspects structure.

Purity tends toward removal.

Container thinking tends toward repair, replacement, relocation, reinforcement, and fit.

This does not mean every boundary is good. A container can imprison. A wall can harm. A category can crush.

But container language at least keeps the question on structure instead of pretending that worth lives inside a stain.

A fast field card:

```text
MILK_DRAGON_CHECK

What words imply stain, contamination, filth, rot, cleansing, purity, corruption, infection, or removal?

Object swap:
Did actor become substance?
Did behaviour become essence?
Did risk become guilt?
Did contact become contamination?
Did legality become truth?
Did stigma become evidence?

Response narrowing:
What responses remain available?
What responses were erased?

Institutional hook:
Where could this frame enter forms, policy, moderation, care, school, border, clinic, platform, or policing systems?

Victim signal:
Who loses standing if the frame is accepted?

Repair:
Replace purity frame with object, boundary, evidence, consent, veto, container, or material action.
```

The Milk Dragon is present when the conversation stops asking what happened and starts asking how to remove the stain.

## 9. AMPHI: keep domains apart

AMPHI is the domain-separation guard.

It matters because purity metaphor often works by collapsing domains:

```text
law -> truth
risk -> guilt
stigma -> evidence
diagnosis -> identity
contact -> contamination
discomfort -> danger
technical ease -> correctness
```

The repair is separation:

```text
law is not truth
risk is not guilt
stigma is not evidence
diagnosis is not identity
association is not cause
metric success is not object survival
```

This does not erase risk. It lets risk be handled as risk instead of being smuggled into blame, stain, or essence.


### REFRI

Block proxy substitution.

```text
SUB_RISK: person -> contaminant
SUB_RISK: witness -> reputational risk
SUB_RISK: policy question -> hygiene question
SUB_RISK: edge case -> noise
```

Then return to the nearest valid object.

## 11. Non-claims

This paper does not claim that every purity-coded word causes violence.

It does not claim that every use of terms like clean, clear, pure, contaminated, or corrupt is morally equivalent.

It does not claim that hygiene, infection control, food safety, or real contamination risk should be ignored.

It does not claim that all boundaries are bad.

It does claim that when purity metaphor attaches to human worth, group identity, victim credibility, stigmatized behaviour, or institutional classification, it deserves careful handling.

The question is not whether a word is banned.

The question is whether the word changed the object.

## 12. What to do today

A practical response:

```text
1. Replace purity language with concrete operation.
2. Name the object before changing it.
3. Separate risk from guilt.
4. Separate law from truth.
5. Separate stigma from evidence.
6. Preserve victim signal.
7. Ask who benefits when the target becomes a stain.
8. Track where the frame enters institutions.
9. Use container language when boundary and repair are the real issue.
10. Refuse the proxy when the person becomes contamination.
```

Examples:

```text
Instead of:
clean the dataset

Say:
deduplicate records, preserve edge cases, document removed fields, record who benefits and who loses information.

Instead of:
remove bad elements

Say:
identify harmful acts, keep persons distinct from acts, preserve due process, define the boundary.

Instead of:
protect the community from contamination

Say:
name the actual risk, evidence, affected parties, reversible supports, vetoes, and safeguards.
```

## 13. Short version

The Milk Dragon is the purity metaphor when it becomes an eater.

It eats by converting people into stains, witnesses into risks, edge cases into noise, and policy questions into hygiene questions.

It is not slain by pretending danger does not exist.

It is slain by refusing the object swap.

```text
Do not ask who is dirty.
Ask what object was lost.
Do not ask what must be cleansed.
Ask what boundary failed.
Do not ask who carries the stain.
Ask who is being eaten by the metaphor.
```

## 14. Closing

Purity metaphor is old.

It is attractive because it is fast. It promises a world where danger can be located in a stain and removed.

But the world is not repaired by moving the stain onto people.

The work is slower:

```text
preserve the object
name the eater
separate the frames
protect the witness
repair the container
block the proxy
```

That is how the Milk Dragon loses cover.

Not by one heroic strike.

By a million careful words.
