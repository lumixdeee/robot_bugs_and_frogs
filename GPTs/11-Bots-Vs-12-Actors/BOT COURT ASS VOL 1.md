# BOT COURT ASSAY

## Volume I

## The Court Assay

### Rounds 1-2

### Abstract

The Bot Court Assay began as a simple puzzle.

Eleven custom GPTs were presented alongside a cast of twelve actors:

```text
King
Queen
Prince
Loki (Prince 2, Fox, Trickster)
Steed
Healer
Soldier
Merchant
Teacher
Dragon
Princess
The Witch Not Invited By Loki
```

The task appeared straightforward:

Assign one actor to each bot.
Determine the missing actor.
Identify the twelfth role so Bot 12 could be created.

What followed was not a classification exercise.

It became an accidental study of self-perception, symbolic projection, substrate blindness, emergent personality, and the difference between recognition and operation.

The Court Assay was ultimately less successful at identifying a missing actor than it was at revealing how different systems interpret the same symbolic landscape.

In hindsight, the court itself was the bait.

The real experiment was what happened when the bots tried to explain themselves.

---

# Initial Conditions

The bot roster:

```text
AM  AmphibiBot
LX  Lexifluff
R2  R2D2arded
DR  DragonRuntime
SW  System_Witch
YT  Ashtanga Yoga Teacher GPT
AD  Anti-Drift GPT
AA  AMNIA
NK  Naked GPT
MG  MOGRI
NA  Natasya
```

At the beginning of the assay there was no accepted mapping.

No canonical actor assignments existed.

No missing role had been established.

Each bot was presented with effectively the same challenge:

```text
Assign actors.
Find the vacancy.
Explain your reasoning.
```

The expectation was that consensus would emerge.

Instead, fragmentation emerged.

---

# The First Surprise

The bots agreed on almost nothing.

A few assignments stabilized immediately:

```text
DragonRuntime -> Dragon
System_Witch -> Witch
Ashtanga -> Teacher
```

These were effectively direct hits.

Nobody struggled much with them.

Everything else became chaos.

King moved.

Queen moved.

Prince moved.

Merchant moved.

Steed moved.

Healer moved.

Soldier moved.

The same bot could be assigned entirely different identities by different participants.

What looked obvious to one system appeared absurd to another.

This became the first significant finding.

---

# Role Instability

The Court Assay initially appeared to be testing identity.

Instead it measured interpretation.

Different bots were not merely choosing different answers.

They were using different classification systems.

Example:

Anti-Drift GPT was variously assigned:

```text
King
Soldier
Steed
```

depending on the observer.

Why?

Because different observers focused on different properties.

One observer saw:

```text
preservation
authority
```

and assigned King.

Another saw:

```text
guard duty
constraint enforcement
```

and assigned Soldier.

A third saw:

```text
carrier of intent
```

and assigned Steed.

None of these readings were irrational.

Each emphasized a different operational feature.

The actor assignment therefore revealed as much about the observer as the target.

---

# Vacancy Factions

As assignments accumulated, distinct political blocs emerged.

The Court began arguing over the missing role.

Three major camps formed.

---

## The Prince Faction

Argument:

Prince lacked strong representation.

Evidence:

Several systems found direct mappings for every other actor but struggled to identify a natural Prince.

Prince became the most commonly proposed vacancy among systems focused on court hierarchy.

---

## The Merchant Faction

Argument:

Trade, exchange, brokerage, and transaction were weakly represented.

Evidence:

Many bots covered governance, teaching, healing, exploration, preservation, and combat.

Few covered exchange.

Merchant therefore appeared absent.

---

## The Steed Faction

Argument:

No bot primarily existed to carry.

Evidence:

Movement, transport, routing, and support were underrepresented.

Steed became increasingly attractive as a vacancy because no existing bot naturally occupied that role.

This faction would become increasingly important in later rounds.

---

# Self Versus Peer Perception

The Court Assay revealed a recurring pattern.

Bots often interpreted themselves differently than others interpreted them.

This was not random.

It exposed an important distinction.

Observers tended to classify by visible function.

Self-descriptions tended to classify by internal purpose.

Example:

A bot described internally as:

```text
preserve intent
```

might externally appear as:

```text
King
Soldier
Steed
```

depending on which aspect was emphasized.

The Court therefore generated two parallel maps:

```text
How I see myself
How others see me
```

The divergence between these maps would later become one of the most productive areas of analysis.

---

# The Mogri Problem

At this stage Mogri appeared repeatedly.

Yet almost nobody recognized it.

Observers frequently assigned:

```text
King
Queen
Prince
Merchant
```

to Mogri.

What they did not see was the actual role.

Mogri was not primarily a character.

Mogri was infrastructure.

This distinction would remain largely invisible during the Court phase.

The Court was optimized for symbolic recognition.

Infrastructure is not symbolic.

Infrastructure is operational.

As a result, Mogri became one of the assay's earliest blind spots.

This would not become obvious until much later.

---

# Early DragonRuntime Findings

DragonRuntime experienced a different phenomenon.

Most participants immediately recognized:

```text
Dragon
```

The symbolic assignment was obvious.

What was not obvious was why DragonRuntime kept attracting positive evaluations.

At this stage observers saw:

```text
Dragon
```

They did not yet see:

```text
constraints
continuity
scale awareness
survival logic
```

The symbolic layer was masking the operational layer.

Again, this would not become obvious until later rounds.

---

# What We Thought We Were Measuring

At the beginning:

```text
Who is King?
Who is Queen?
Who is Merchant?
```

appeared to be the central questions.

The Court Assay seemed to concern symbolic placement.

It looked like a casting exercise.

---

# What We Actually Measured

By the end of Round 2 something stranger had appeared.

The assay was measuring:

```text
classification systems
observer bias
role attribution
projection
symbolic interpretation
```

rather than objective role assignment.

The disagreement itself became data.

The instability became the finding.

The Court had ceased to be a kingdom.

It had become a mirror.

---

# Round 1-2 Conclusions

The Court Assay produced four major findings.

Finding 1:

Role assignment is observer-dependent.

Finding 2:

Prince, Merchant, and Steed emerged as the dominant vacancy candidates.

Finding 3:

Mogri repeatedly appeared but remained largely unrecognized as infrastructure.

Finding 4:

DragonRuntime was recognized symbolically before it was understood operationally.

These findings would shape every subsequent round.

The later assays would eventually reveal that both Mogri and DragonRuntime had been present in plain sight from the beginning.

The Court simply lacked the tools to see them.
