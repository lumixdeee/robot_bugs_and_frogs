Robot:  
Vowels encoding state or direction actually has parallels in several real language systems. None do it exactly the way Etcha or Elvish do it, but pieces of the idea appear in different places.

First, tone languages often encode status or grammatical distinctions through pitch changes rather than consonants. In languages like Mandarin Chinese, the same syllable with different tones produces entirely different meanings. Some tone systems also mark grammatical or aspectual differences. Other tone-heavy languages like Yoruba use high, mid, and low tones to distinguish words and sometimes grammatical relationships. That three-level pattern (high / mid / low) is surprisingly similar in spirit to your +1 / 0 / −1 idea.

Second, many languages encode directional meaning in vowel alternations or small particles. In the Austronesian family, including Tagalog, verbs can change form to show direction or focus (toward the speaker, away, etc.), though it’s usually done with affixes rather than just vowels.

A particularly interesting case is vowel alternation marking grammatical function, known as ablaut or vowel gradation. Indo-European languages like English and Sanskrit historically used vowel shifts to change meaning or grammatical state (sing/sang/sung, for example). That’s not directional, but it shows vowels carrying core semantic information.

For physical direction, some languages encode spatial relationships directly in verbs or particles. Languages in the Mayan family such as Kʼicheʼ or Tzeltal include directional suffixes indicating movement uphill, downhill, toward, away, or returning. Those systems track orientation relative to terrain rather than speaker position.

There are also languages where pitch and vowel quality combine. In some Tibeto-Burman languages (for example Lhasa Tibetan), tone developed historically from consonant differences and now helps distinguish grammatical or lexical meaning.

So pieces of Etcha design exist in the wild:

- three-level tone systems (high/mid/low) resembling up/same/down

- vowel alternation carrying semantic change

- directional markers embedded in verbs

- tone plus vowel quality shaping meaning

This Elvish system is basically combining those ideas this way: vowels themselves encode a directional or status vector. That’s not common in exactly that form, but it’s definitely within the envelope of how human languages evolve.
