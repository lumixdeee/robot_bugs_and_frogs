Gobekli Tepe and the First-Perceived-Wealth Problem

Protected Processing, Visible Resistance, and Demographic Pressure at the Farming Hinge

Working paper v0.97 | journal-readiness draft | 21 May 2026

Abstract

This paper does not argue that Gobekli Tepe was mainly a fort, animal trap, coercive labor extraction site, hunting office, or storage compound. Version 0.97 moves the protected-processing model toward journal readiness by tightening its material anchor, lifecycle sequence, and test conditions. The model begins from an observed positive pattern: the unusually large ground-stone processing assemblage in a monumental, ritualised setting.

The core reading remains ritual-social: gathering, feasting, death practice, T-pillars as stone persons, animal imagery, social identity, and regional symbolic network. The added layer is first-perceived-wealth raider-risk. The vulnerable object was not only food. It was the working person embedded in a processing sequence. Protected processing therefore guarded both food value and labouring bodies at the moment when value was being made.

The demographic layer does not require a crisis. Early Holocene abundance could raise carrying capacity, allowing more people, heavier grain draw, and sharper competition around visible cereal processing. Successful bread-making and storage can multiply their users, reduce fallback space for non-storing mobile groups, and make processing nodes worth watching even without exceptional famine.

Version 0.97 adds three journal-facing layers. First, everyday life may have remained largely perishable and fire-vulnerable while value-dense processing and gathering moments were selectively hardened in stone. Second, regional T-pillar sites may have operated as walking-distance service nodes rather than as colonies of one centre. Third, the burial or covering of such places may mark self-directed memory management after outside competitors became guests, kin, partners, and participants.

The behavioural sequence remains simple: B1 feels safer, B1 repeats, M1 tests, M1 fails, M1 remembers, and the visible form inherits the bite. The T-pillar tradition need not begin as a formal warning system. It can become a copied visible system of hard, chronic resistance because failed approaches teach competitors that workers in such places are not alone.

Anti-misread note

The model does not propose that herds were driven into the central enclosures, that T-pillars were fencing posts, that Gobekli Tepe was built to capture labourers, or that ritual was a cover story for economics. The central enclosures remain the ritual core. Any tactical affordance belongs to approaches, thresholds, outer routes, terraces, timber additions, temporary screens, food-adjacent barriers, and feeder-landscape movement, and remains speculative unless field data supports it.

Version 0.97 distinguishes three person-risk claims. It does not claim that the site captured outsiders for labour. It does claim that exposed grain workers in the wider landscape could be seized, coerced, followed, or used to locate stores. It also claims that a protected processing node could make such attacks costly by ensuring that the worker was not alone, unseen, or unsupported.

Version 0.97 also avoids a stone-town misread. The model expects asymmetry: soft everyday structures may leave faint traces, while dangerous communal and processing nodes leave durable stone evidence. It also avoids a concealment-only reading of burial. Covering a site may be self-directed memory management, not necessarily denial of knowledge to outsiders.

2. Site baseline and evidence floor

Gobekli Tepe lies in the Germus mountains of south-eastern Anatolia. UNESCO dates its monumental round-oval and rectangular megalithic buildings to the Pre-Pottery Neolithic, roughly 9600-8200 BCE, and frames them as hunter-gatherer communal monuments probably tied to social events and rituals, most likely funerary. Some T-shaped limestone pillars reach up to 5.5 m. [1]

The cereal-processing study gives measurable anchors: monumental buildings about 10-20 m in inner diameter, central pillars up to 5.5 m, and more than 7000 grinding and pounding artifacts. Phytolith analysis supports massive cereal presence, while large storage facilities were not identified in that study; the authors favour seasonal large work feasts. [2]

This unusually large processing assemblage is the primary positive material footprint of the protected-processing model. In a more dispersed processing pattern, grain handling would be expected closer to harvest points and exposed work spots. At Gobekli Tepe, heavy processing equipment appears aggregated in a monumental, topographically managed setting. That pattern can be read as a practical shift from dispersed exposed processing toward collective, visible, protected processing.

The evidence floor also includes wild animal remains, animal imagery, direct hunting evidence, scattered human fragments, modified skull material, complex fill histories, and regional T-pillar parallels. These facts support the ritual-social core, the food-processing layer, and the hunt/feast competence layer. They do not, by themselves, prove raider-risk deterrence.

3. Soft dwelling worlds and hardened value nodes

Gobekli Tepe need not imply a stone-built domestic world. Ordinary occupation may have relied heavily on perishable shelters, mud, wattle, timber, reeds, hides, matting, baskets, screens, and vegetal roofing. Such materials are archaeologically fragile and strategically vulnerable, especially to fire.

The asymmetry is therefore important. Everyday life could remain soft, repairable, seasonal, and partly dispersed, while the dangerous, value-dense moments of processing, gathering, and display became selectively hardened in stone. The durable archaeological footprint is not necessarily where everyone lived all the time. It may instead be the hardened answer to soft dwelling and exposed processing.

This helps explain why small isolated B1 dwelling sites may be sparse, short-lived, or hard to identify. A dispersed shelter cluster with vegetal roofs, doors, matting, storage baskets, fuel, racks, fences, and screens is vulnerable to fire visits and nighttime pressure. Stone does not need to replace ordinary life everywhere. It only needs to harden the moments and places where worker exposure, food value, and group memory converge.

4. Early Holocene pressure: abundance without management

Gobekli Tepe appears almost immediately after the end of the last glacial period in archaeological terms. The formal Holocene boundary is about 11.7 ka before present, while the earliest secure monumental phases at Gobekli Tepe fall in the later tenth millennium BCE. [1], [15] The landscape was not identical to the modern setting. The relevant model is a somewhat wetter early Holocene steppe: wild cereals, grazing animals, seasonal water, and local tree resources, not a dense forest and not a stable breadbasket. [16]

Rising carrying capacity does not remove scarcity pressure. It can postpone and amplify it. Wetter conditions, wild cereal stands, and richer game populations could support more people, but population growth would not automatically produce regional coordination, storage equity, conflict management, or winter provisioning. Abundance allows population to rise toward a new ceiling; it does not guarantee safe access for everyone at the hungry moment.

The model should never depend on crisis. Seasonal failure, hard winters, disease, missed hunts, drought pockets, and broken exchange ties would intensify pressure, but they are not required. If bread-making and grain storage succeeded, groups using them could increase in number and return with greater demand on the same wild cereal stands. Their success would gradually reduce the grain available to groups still relying on older mobile fallback systems.

Neighbouring groups would not remain passive. Once bread-making and storage became visible, non-storing mobile groups could copy, join, intermarry, exchange, steal tools, or learn the practice directly. The technical advantage of grinding grain into food may therefore have begun to erode quickly. Gobekli Tepe does not need to explain the invention of bread-making. It needs to explain the organisation of bread-making under competitive conditions.

Local population increase may also have combined with people, practices, and partners moving from regions to the south with longer cereal-processing traditions. Earlier grinders or bread-like products elsewhere do not, by themselves, establish a migration route or direction of influence. They demonstrate technical possibility. The relevant question is where processing happened, at what scale, under which social rules, and with what risk. [13], [14], [17]

This is not a claim that incoming or southern-linked groups were inherently weak. The relevant difference is ecological familiarity. Local groups may have had more practice with the winter fallback routes of this particular landscape, while people connected to more cereal-practised zones may have made new forms of value visible through grinding, smoke, cooked food, tools, and processed grain. In severe scarcity, internal conspecific consumption would carry catastrophic social cost; external seizure of people associated with processed grain would remain dangerous and revenge-prone, but could appear less destructive to the attacker's own social body.

5. The stationary problem: capability was not enough

Hunting was not the broken system. People already knew animal routes, seasonal timing, danger, tools, group movement, carcass processing, and feast logistics. Early agriculture should also not be treated as a sudden discovery of a thing humans had previously been unable to imagine. People could know where cereals grew, how they ripened, how to harvest and process them, how to store some foods, how animals bred, and how to build walls or fences without yet possessing a durable farming system.

The hard problem was stationary system integration. A group trying to settle had to keep older support systems alive while investing labour in fixed plant stands, processing equipment, buildings, storage, water, repair, dogs, social gathering, and defence. The new system could consume labour before it reliably returned surplus. The group could become visible before it became safe: too settled to vanish easily, not yet buffered by mature farming, and known enough to attract hungry outsiders.

Stationary life also changes the arithmetic of shelter and work. A hundred detached single-room houses require roughly 400 wall-lengths. A 10 by 10 block of attached rooms needs about 220 wall-lengths because walls are shared. This is not a claim about a specific Gobekli Tepe housing plan. It is a scale reminder: clustering near processing, water, watch, storage, dogs, tools, and ritual authority can be driven by mundane wall economy and walking economy as much as by ideology.

Monumentality is the counter-signal. Before accounts, vaults, or bank balances, visible organised capacity is the signal of negotiable value. Raising stone persons, hosting feasts, coordinating labour, maintaining ritual authority, and drawing visitors all say that the group has something to bargain with, something to defend, and allies or obligations beyond a single household. The same broadcast that makes the site attractive also warns that it is not an easy food pile.

6. Protected processing and the labour-theft payoff

The first-perceived-wealth problem is sharpened by processing. Grain in a stand has value. Harvested grain has more value because labour has been added. Flour has still more value because harvesting, transport, dehusking, pounding, grinding, tool wear, and fatigue have been folded into it. Theft of flour steals calories and labour together.

The highest-risk object was not only food. It was the working person inside the food-making sequence. A tired grinder or baker may carry skill, tools, grain access, route knowledge, social information, and possible knowledge of stores. A group approaching such a person does not need to be a specialist raiding class. Opportunistic attackers could control the worker first, then take flour, tools, stores, or directions.

This makes dispersed processing more dangerous than simple calorie theft suggests. Sneak theft can become confrontation under bad conditions. A thief who only takes bread is the mild case. A group that takes the baker at distance, then takes stores, is the fitter threat model. Protected communal processing therefore guarded food value, labour value, and the bodies that held both.

The unusually large and standardised grinding assemblage at Gobekli Tepe may therefore matter not only as feast evidence, but as evidence of protected conversion. It is a positive material trace of processing being pulled into a place where labour, tools, food, witnesses, ritual attention, and route control could converge.

The raider-risk layer need not begin with organised enemies. It may begin with observation. A mobile group can learn that grain gatherers become more vulnerable after harvest, when grain is carried, processed, and converted into flour or bread-like food. If those opportunists then observe that processing no longer happens at scattered vulnerable locations but increasingly at Gobekli Tepe, the site becomes worth testing. In this model, the first attackers are not a formal raider class. They are people whose profitable theft pattern has been disrupted by protected processing.

A protected processing node also creates contribution logic without requiring a later state tax. People may bring grain, grind near allies and watchers, take processed food away, and return a share as feast support, labour obligation, ritual contribution, or social debt. The site value lies not only in what it stored, but in what it made safer to process.

7. External pressure and the feeder landscape

The model should not expect the strongest attack evidence at Gobekli Tepe itself. If the site was a successful response to the first-perceived-wealth problem, then the triggering pressure may be better sought in the surrounding landscape: smaller processing stations, seasonal camps, minor stores, caches, grain-working zones, and feeder settlements. Gobekli Tepe is not the cause of Gobekli Tepe. It is the architectural and ritual answer to a risk first generated elsewhere.

This claim needs strict testing. A feeder landscape cannot become a hiding place for the hypothesis. Evidence outside the monumental core would strengthen the model only if several unrelated stress indicators converge in space and time: damaged or repaired bins, abrupt abandonment of food-working areas, moved or missing grinding equipment, impact-diagnostic stone fragments near access routes, localised burning, route-facing patching, and movement from dispersed processing toward protected communal processing.

Single indicators are not enough. Ordinary seasonal movement, tool recycling, refuse movement, erosion, shifting foraging ranges, later disturbance, and formation processes remain better explanations unless the pattern is clustered and route-relevant. The model is weakened if feeder sites show vulnerable processing without stress, storage pressure, route change, or movement toward protected nodes.

The worker-risk layer makes the feeder landscape more important. Stores can be hidden or emptied. A tired worker on a return route, at a grinding hollow, or near a minor cache is harder to protect alone. This predicts evidence of relocation, paired processing, watched paths, tool movement, or increasing attraction toward communal food-work nodes rather than a simple fortress signature.

8. Event-rate realism and the adjustment threshold

The model does not require repeated successful raids. It requires stress events, occasional attempts, and remembered failure. Across a long use-life and a regional gathering catchment, disrupted theft patterns, worker pursuit, coercion, bad seasons, failed hunts, disease, injuries, and broken exchange relations could generate raider pressure many times without producing many battle layers at the core site.

Protected processing need not originate in a single crisis. It may instead reflect accumulated adjustment after successful cereal processing created repeated exposure. Groups using bread-making and storage gained real advantages, but those advantages made workers, tools, routes, and processed food more visible. Even limited theft, coercion, pursuit, or worker loss could make dispersed processing too costly.

The response was not simply more food storage. It was a reorganisation of the processing moment itself: fewer isolated workers, more witnesses, tighter routes, stronger group presence, and a public memory that attacks on food work would not remain private incidents.

One early incident could teach the resident group where the system nearly failed. A later failed attempt could create warning memory. After that, most of the work may be done by reputation, guest-role conversion, avoidance, bargaining, joining, or aborted approaches. The stone does not need to fall often if the story keeps falling.

9. Copied visible system and learned resistance

Boar and deer are dangerous prey, but they are not dangerous in the same social way. They can injure or kill. They cannot conduct revenge, remember a grievance across kin groups, or turn a single attack into a lasting relation between communities. A human worker is different. To seize that person is to touch a remembered social body.

The copied visible system need not have begun as rule-making or formal warning architecture. For the protected group, the practical behaviour may have been simple: workers were safer near other people, near dogs, near stone, near witnesses, and within managed routes. The arrangement is repeated because it works.

The warning value emerges on the other side of the encounter. B1 feels safer, B1 repeats, M1 tests, M1 fails, M1 remembers, and the visible form inherits the bite. M1 learns that the T-pillar world marks a place where coercion, theft, or attack meets hard, chronic resistance. The sign is authored less by initial intention than by failed approaches and remembered cost.

Longer-term cost does not require courts, codes, or centralized enforcement. It can work through loss of guest status, denial of feast access, exclusion from coordinated hunts or seasonal gatherings, partner-exchange refusal, lithic or symbolic exchange loss, retaliation memory, and public naming as an unsafe faction. In this sense, the T-pillar tradition is better framed as a copied visible system of hard chronic resistance than as proto-law.

10. T-pillars, thresholds, and close-up mechanics

The central enclosures are the ritual core, not the proposed combat chamber. Their inward-facing design, central stone persons, benches, animal imagery, and death-practice associations support gathering, memory, and attention. Any tactical affordance should be looked for in approaches, threshold zones, terraces, outer routes, temporary timber, screens, and food-adjacent barriers.

The threshold effect should not depend mainly on guessing attacker psychology. The stronger claim is spatial. A narrow approach, slope, watched route, dog noise, stone edge, resident clustering, and limited entry width can let a smaller resident group equalise a numbers disadvantage. The problem is not that attackers magically fear a T-pillar. The problem is that attackers who must approach through a charged and observed route lose surprise, lose spread, and meet residents where residents can gather.

No builder needs to have said, "we will erect T-pillars to warn them off," and no attacker needs to have fled merely because a T-pillar was seen. The defensive significance lies in the mechanical and social conditions produced by the architecture. Stone architecture reduces the value of ordinary missile attack and offers little obvious reward to fire-setting tactics. If an aggressor must then approach, the site can shift the encounter into a narrow, visible, low-error-margin space. Over time, the copied form can come to mean: expect hard chronic resistance.

A massive upright stone is beyond the scale of an individual weapon. It is stored height, stored weight, and stored instability. This is not proof that T-pillars were used tactically. It is a low-rank mechanical possibility: a stone too heavy for one person to wield can still become decisive if architecture, slope, threshold, and timing make falling stone part of the encounter.

The most limited version does not require destroying the main T-pillars. A separate upper stone, perhaps similar in form to the upper part of a T-pillar, could be positioned above a route, threshold, or support. Such a stone would remain beyond the scale of a hand weapon, yet could become decisive if released, shoved, or allowed to fall at close approach. This remains speculative unless evidence appears for perched stones, sockets, displaced upper blocks, impact zones, odd repair, or route-linked rubble.

14. Projectiles: incoming, salvaged, and low-rank

The model does not predict many outgoing defender projectiles. In the delay-and-drop version, defenders wait until attackers are delayed, bunched, and attention-locked, then use height, stone, timber, dogs, clubs, axes, and reroute pressure. If projectile evidence exists, it may come mainly from attackers carrying or firing stone points into the site.

Usable flint or obsidian points would likely be recovered, inspected, reworked, or kept. The traces to seek are not neat arrowheads left for a modern display case. Better targets are broken tips missed in fill, embedded fragments, access-zone impact damage, route-specific micro-debitage, stone-edge spalling, and injury evidence. Even these remain low-rank unless mapped against ordinary knapping, trampling, grinding, and fill processes.

15. Regional network, walking-distance nodes, Karahan Tepe, and T-pillar echo

Gobekli Tepe should not be treated as an isolated target. Regional T-pillar sites and related symbolic forms suggest a broader landscape of shared monument language, visitors, allies, and emulation. A raider-mode group approaching one node may be approaching a network of kin, guests, exchange partners, and ritual obligations rather than a lone pantry.

Other T-pillar sites need not share the original GT problem or layout. Once the visible form acquires a history of hard chronic resistance, related sites can adopt it as prestige echo, coalition signalling, regional identity claim, or participation marker. The copy need not be false. It is how the bite travels.

The chronological relation between Karahan Tepe and Gobekli Tepe remains important but not decisive for the model. Karahan Tepe could represent an earlier, parallel, or later expression of the same regional visible system. If earlier, it may show that range resistance, siege resistance, and monumental signalling were already available before the close-approach problem was more fully solved at Gobekli Tepe. If parallel, the two sites may preserve different local solutions inside the same pressure field. If later, Karahan Tepe may inherit a monument form whose close-approach cost had already entered regional memory. For now, the KT/GT sequence should remain an open variable rather than a forced premise.

A useful analogy is an army. Only some members are veterans with direct experience of the event-world that formed the discipline. Many others are recruits: dressed, drilled, and arranged to reproduce the same posture before they have the same history. Likewise, one site may preserve a high-pressure solution to close approach, while related sites inherit the monument language, social posture, and learned cost without preserving every mechanical affordance.

The spread of T-pillar sites may reflect a regional mesh of locally scaled aggregation nodes rather than centralized expansion from a single origin. Their spacing should be expected to follow walking catchments, resource basins, approach routes, and population territories. A node too distant cannot protect workers, host repeated processing, or carry warning memory effectively; a node too near another may duplicate costly labour.

Acceptable walking distance is not a fixed radius. It depends on movement under load: carrying grain, moving tools, escorting workers, bringing children or elders, reaching witnesses before danger peaks, and returning before darkness, fatigue, or spoilage changes the risk. Similar architecture could therefore emerge near-synchronously as neighbouring groups faced the same exposure problem. This keeps the KT/GT sequence open: earlier, parallel, or later relationships can all remain possible until the field sequence is better constrained.

16. End-state, integration, and burial

The endpoint of this model is not permanent opposition between processors and outsiders. Over time, neighbouring groups could copy the processing system, enter exchange relations, marry into the network, become regular guests, or participate in shared gatherings. In that end-state, M1 is no longer simply outside B1. The original opposition weakens because the once-external pressure has been absorbed into the social field.

When that happens, the visible system of hard chronic resistance no longer needs to perform the same work. The stone node may remain powerful, but its old job has changed. Covering or burying such places need not be read only as concealment from others. It may also represent self-directed memory management: the community no longer needed to live beside a monumental reminder of the coercive, fearful, or retaliatory conditions under which the system first became necessary.

This interpretation should remain cautious. Burial can have many causes: rebuilding, stabilisation, ritual closure, sediment movement, memory practice, or practical site management. The point is only that denial for outsiders is not required. A community may also cover a place because descendants no longer want the old hard-resistance form to dominate everyday life.

17. Low-rank mechanisms, kept short

Delay-and-drop remains a tactical affordance, not a proven event: a visible or scent-linked threshold delays the front of a small raiding group, spatial routing produces a pause, and defenders use height or side positions to break momentum. This is a proposed use of architecture, not a demonstrated fact.

Falling-stone or upper-stone mechanisms remain lower-rank than protected processing, worker-risk, feeder-landscape testing, and copied visible resistance. They should stay in appendix or brief mechanism notes unless timber, support, socket, route, rubble, repair, or impact evidence supports them.

Mortuary crisis roles and rare emergency conspecific consumption are also edge cases. Modified skull fragments are best treated first as death practice and body handling; scarcity readings remain theoretical limit-cases, not ordinary practice.

18.2. Conditions that would weaken the model

The protected-processing model is tied to material boundaries evaluated through likelihood rather than a binary proof test. It would be substantially weakened if future survey and excavation found durable, unprotected, isolated large-scale grain-processing or processed-food storage installations with long-term use, no movement toward communal nodes, no route control, and no spatial relationship to monumental centres.

It would also be weakened by a lack of convergent stress-wear, high-intensity fracture usage, accelerated repair, or restricted-window use patterning on the ground-stone assemblage. A third weakening condition would be evidence that contemporary T-pillar sites are distributed without relation to approach routes, visibility, choke points, or resource basins.

19. Figure plan for v1.0: three-zone spatial matrix

Figure 1 should model three zones rather than a single undifferentiated site map. Zone 1 is the exposed feeder landscape: wild grain stands, harvesting pathways, minor ephemeral storage caches, initial grain handling, and isolated workers carrying high-fatigue labour loads. This is the high-exposure, low-structure zone.

Zone 2 is the warning threshold: topographic compression, approach queues, terrace lines, screens, high mutual visibility, resident clustering, and domestic dogs capable of converting stealth or ambush into acoustic notice where food, people, scraps, and sleeping bodies cluster.

Zone 3 is the monumental core: T-pillar enclosures, ritual display, coalition presence, communal spaces, and the unusually large aggregated processing toolkit. Direct traces of physical violence are expected to be lower here than in feeder routes and threshold zones because the core functions as the strongest counter-signal.

The map rule is temporal fit: geography must match the period being argued. Modern rivers, modern greenery, and modern settlement logic should not be allowed to substitute for early Holocene conditions. The figure should mark uncertainty rather than pretending to solve it.

A second figure or inset should show the regional mesh: local nodes spaced by walking catchments, resource basins, likely routes, and visibility fields. This is not a map of central control. It is a map of reachable hard nodes serving local populations.

A third optional schematic can show the lifecycle: exposed processing, selective hardening, copied visible form, M1 learning through failed approach, integration, and burial as retirement of a former necessity.

20. Public and academic language note

Version 0.97 keeps the main text close to ordinary archaeological language. The v1.0 pass should continue that translation without changing the paper spine: do not let one known role swallow another, do not turn absence into proof too quickly, and keep speculative mechanisms in rank until data supports them.

For v1.0, earlier local vocabulary can be mapped onto standard terms such as underdetermination, equifinality, formation processes, missing perishable material, partial evidence, confidence ranking, and premature explanatory closure. The human-review branch should read like archaeology first and project terminology second.

The internal shorthand can remain offstage in publication prose. The archaeological translation is enough: repeated practical safety behaviour can become regional meaning after failed approaches teach competitors that a visible form marks hard chronic resistance.

Journal readiness requires two habits. First, keep the positive material anchor ahead of speculative mechanisms. Second, translate local project terms into standard archaeological language before submission while preserving the behavioural sequence that gives the paper its spine.

Conclusion

Version 0.97 moves the protected-processing model toward journal readiness by adding lifecycle and infrastructure. The thesis is no longer a trap claim. It is a protected-processing claim inside a ritual-social system, anchored in the unusually large ground-stone assemblage at Gobekli Tepe and tested through spatial, use-wear, feeder-landscape, and regional distribution predictions.

The paper should not depend on crisis. Hard winters, failed seasons, disease, and missed hunts could intensify pressure, but the base mechanism is ordinary success under weak coordination. Successful bread-making and storage can raise population, increase grain draw, shrink older fallback space, and make visible processing worth watching. Imitation then erodes the first technical advantage. The durable advantage lies in organisation: protected workers, controlled routes, witnesses, guest conversion, and repeated visible form.

The behavioural sequence is simple: B1 feels safer, B1 repeats, M1 tests, M1 fails, M1 remembers, and the form inherits the bite. Site food events, smoke, grinding, visitors, monumentality, and regional node position could make the system visible to hungry outsiders. Its ritual core, stone persons, threshold routes, coalition signal, safe processing role, and outer-route affordances could convert many outsiders into guests, witnesses, allies, or warned rivals before violence formed.

The v0.97 addition is that the durable stone footprint may be the hard part of a mostly soft world. Perishable dwellings, vegetal roofs, mats, baskets, doors, fences, fuel, racks, and screens could remain everyday infrastructure while high-risk processing and gathering nodes became selectively hardened. Regional T-pillar sites may then reflect reachable service nodes spaced by walking catchments rather than a single centre exporting doctrine.

The end-state is absorption rather than permanent war. As M1 groups copy, join, marry in, trade in, and participate, the hard-resistance node loses its original role. Burial or covering can therefore be read as possible self-directed memory management: the community no longer needs to live beside the visible teeth of an old necessity.

Appendix A. Low-rank taphonomic and competitor notes

Bone is not proposed as primary raider bait. In the main argument it should be treated only as taphonomic and resident delayed-value context: marrow, mineral, tool stock, animal feed, ritual matter, trace-loss pathway, and food-scent source. Bone fragments and food waste can attract suids, canids, vultures, and other scavengers into known observation zones. This may help explain why traces move, vanish, or acquire gnaw marks, but it is not a main explanation for the site. [18-20]

Large carnivores such as bears should remain an appendix-grade competitor note. A wounded bear is not dependable meat unless it dies nearby. The value of shooting or displacing such an animal may be competitor reduction around nuts, honey, carrion, stores, or processing zones. This remains an opportunistic scarcity-ecology note, not a core part of the protected-processing model.
